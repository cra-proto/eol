RRSPs

How a Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) is treated and reported at the time of the plan holder's (annuitant) death depends on whether it is unmatured or matured and if there was a rollover or transfer to a survivor or beneficiary.

You may need to contact the financial institution or investment broker of the person who died to determine which type of RRSP they held.

Unmatured RRSP

Reporting RRSP income depends on how it is transferred

After the annuitant’s death, the RRSP can be transferred to a spouse or common-law partner, other qualifying survivors or beneficiaries, or the estate. Income is reported differently depending on how it is transferred.

Determine how the RRSP is being transferred

Who is the unmatured RRSP being transferred to?

The spouse or common-law partner as the sole beneficiary
Eligibility for special treatment for a full transfer to a spouse or common-law partner

If the full amount is transferred to the spouse or common-law partner, the RRSP is not reported on the deceased annuitant's final return 70(1).

To be eligible, 2 conditions must be met:

  • The spouse or common-law partner is named as sole beneficiary of the RRSP contract or the will
  • The entire RRSP property is directly transferred, by December 31 of the year following the year of death, to either:
    • the RRSP, PRPP, SPP, or RRIF in which the spouse or common-law partner is the annuitant
    • an issuer to buy an eligible annuity for the spouse or common-law partner

If the full amount is not transferred solely to the spouse or common-law partner, or if the transfer doesn't happen by December 31 of the year following the death, the RRSP will be transferred to the spouse as a qualifying survivor instead.

Transfer is not limited by the usual RRSP contribution limit

The transfer to the spouse or common-law partner's RRSP is exempt from their RRSP contribution limit.

When filing their T1 Income Tax and Benefit Return, the spouse or common-law partner should:

  • complete Part C of Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities
  • claim the transfer on line 15 to ensure that the RRSP deduction limit is not reduced by this amount
Report the full transfer
Found on slip
T4RSP - box 18 (issued to the spouse or common-law partner)
Report on line
12900 of the spouse or common-law partner's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

The RRSP of the deceased is not reported on the deceased annuitant's final return 70(1).

The spouse or common-law partner must report the amount transferred as income on their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return, and may be able to claim a deduction for this amount.

Claim the deduction

Claim the amount from box 18 as a deduction on line 20800.

Change in value after the date of death

If there is an increase in the value of the RRSP between the date of death and the transfer, it will be included in the box 18 amount that the spouse or common-law partner reports on line 12900 and 20800.

If there is a decrease in the value of the RRSP between the date of death and the transfer, it will not affect the box 18 amount. Instead, the spouse or common-law partner may claim a line 23200 deduction on the decease in value.

One or more qualifying survivors (such as a spouse or grandchild)
Who is considered a qualifying survivor

A deceased annuitant's spouse or common-law partner or financially-dependent children or grandchildren are considered qualifying survivors.

Which children qualify as financially dependent

A child or grandchild of an annuitant, where before the annuitant's death, were living with them and met 1 of the following conditions:

  • net income for the previous year (line 23600 of income tax return) was less than the basic personal amount (line 30000 of income tax return) for that previous year
  • financial dependence is due to a mental or physical infirmity and your net income for the previous year was equal to or less than the basic personal amount, plus the disability amount (line 31600 of income tax return) for that previous year
Transfer is not limited by usual RRSP contribution limit

The transfer to a qualifying survivor's RRSP is exempt from their RRSP contribution limit.

When filing their T1 Income Tax and Benefit Return, the qualifying survivor should:

  • complete Part C of Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities
  • claim the transfer on line 15 to ensure that the RRSP deduction limit is not reduced by this amount

You may reduce the amount of income that is reported on the deceased's final return 70(1) if amounts from the RRSP are transferred to the registered plans of qualifying survivors.

Report the amount transferred to a qualifying survivor
Found on slip

T4RSP - box 28 (issued to the qualifying survivor)

T4RSP - box 18 (instead of box 28 if the qualifying survivor is the spouse or common-law partner)

Report on line

12900 of the qualifying survivor's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

The qualifying survivor must report the amount transferred in full on line 12900 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return.

Claim a deduction for the transfer to a qualifying survivor

The amount transferred to a qualifying survivor is considered deductible if the transfer:

  • was made to the qualifying survivor's RRSP, PRPP, SPP, RRIF, RDSP or used to buy them an annuity
  • was within the fair market value of the RRSP when the annuitant died plus any income earned in the RRSP before December 31 of the year following the death
  • was made within 60 days of the end of the year that the RRSP was paid out (this is only relevant to indirect transfers, such as transfers with form T2019)
  • was made before December 31 of the year during which the qualifying survivor turns 71

Amounts transferred that meet these criteria are a deductible refund of premiums.

If amounts earned in the RRSP after December 31 of the year following the death were included in a transfer, they might not qualify for the deduction (not considered a refund of premiums). If this is the case, consult your financial institution about amounts that might not qualify.

The qualifying survivor will claim the deduction on the refund of premiums (generally, the same as the amount transferred) on:

  • line 20800 if transferred to their RRSP, PRPP or SPP
  • line 23200 if transferred to their RRIF, RDSP or annuity

Attach the receipt for the transfer of funds issued by the bank or other issuer to the return. This is to ensure the transfer meets the requirements for the deduction.

Change in value after the date of death

If there is an increase in the value of the RRSP between the date of death and the transfer, the increase will be included in the box 28 or box 18 amount that the qualifying survivor reports on line 12900 and claims on the line 20800 or line 23200 deduction.

If there is a decrease in the value of the RRSP between the date of death and the transfer, it will not be included in the box 28 or box 18 amount. Instead, the qualifying survivor may claim a line 23200 deduction on the decease in value.

If the RRSP is not transferred until after December 31 of the year following the death

Income of the RRSP after December 31 of the year following the death is reported differently for different banks and financial institutions. Ask the financial institution managing the RRSP if it is a depositary, trusteed or insured RRSP, and refer to the third column in Chart 1 of the RC4177 Death of an RRSP Annuitant about how to report it.

Amounts earned in a depositary or trusteed RRSP after December 31 of the year following the death do not qualify to be part of the line 20800 or line 23200 deductions.

A child or grandchild with an RDSP (rollover to an RDSP)
Found on slip
T4A - box 131 (issued to the beneficiary)
Report on line
12500 of the beneficiary's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

Amounts transferred to an eligible qualifying survivor's RDSP do not need to be included as income of the deceased on line 12900 of the final return 70(1).

Which qualifying survivors are eligible

A qualifying survivor is eligible for the rollover to an RDSP if they are:

  • a child or grandchild of a deceased annuitant
  • the beneficiary under the RDSP into which the eligible proceeds will be paid
  • financially dependent on the deceased for support, at the time of the deceased's death, because of an impairment in physical or mental function
Which children qualify as financially dependent

A child or grandchild of an annuitant, where before the annuitant's death, were living with them and met 1 of the following conditions:

  • net income for the previous year (line 23600 of income tax return) was less than the basic personal amount (line 30000 of income tax return) for that previous year
  • financial dependence is due to a mental or physical infirmity and your net income for the previous year was equal to or less than the basic personal amount, plus the disability amount (line 31600 of income tax return) for that previous year
Transfer the RRSP amount to the RDSP

The payment into the RDSP must meet all of the following conditions:

  • The payment is made to an RDSP under which the eligible individual is the beneficiary
  • The payment does not cause the RDSP to exceed the $200,000 lifetime limit of the RDSP and is made before the RDSP holder turns 59
  • If the eligible individual no longer qualifies for the disability tax credit, the payment is made before the end of the fifth taxation year throughout which the beneficiary is ineligible for the disability tax credit

You will not have to fill out a Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities.

Claim the deduction

The amount reported on line 12900 of the deceased annuitant's final return 70(1) is reduced by the amount rolled over to the RDSP.

A completed RC4625, Rollover to a Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) Under Paragraph 60(m) must be attached to both the deceased annuitant's and the eligible qualifying survivor's income tax and benefit returns.

You must attach to the income tax and benefit return the receipt from the bank or other issuer for the transfer indicating the amount rolled over.

At the time of the annuitant’s death, we consider the deceased to have received the fair market value of all property held in their unmatured RRSP. This amount will be in box 34 of the T4RSP slip issued to the deceased.

This amount and all other amounts the annuitant received from the RRSP in the year of death have to be reported on line 12900 of the annuitant’s final return 70(1).

Payments to beneficiaries of the estate

As the amount is reported as part of the deceased's final return 70(1), there is no requirement for beneficiaries of amounts paid from the deceased's RRSP to report the amount received in their own tax returns.

If there is no beneficiary, the RRSP property is generally paid out to the deceased annuitant's estate.

Claim a deduction for amounts transferred to a qualifying survivor

If a beneficiary of the estate who receives an amount from the RRSP reported in the final return 70(1) is a qualifying survivor, you may want to consider designating the amount of the RRSP transferred from the estate to the qualifying survivor as a refund of premiums:

  1. The annuitant's legal representative and the qualifying survivor jointly file Form T2019, Death of an RRSP Annuitant- Refund of Premiums
  2. Line 12900 of the deceased's final return 70(1) should be reduced by the amount designated, if the transfer meets the requirements to be considered deductible
  3. The amount transferred to the qualifying survivor is then deducted from income in the qualifying survivor's return, if the transfer meets the requirements to be considered deductible

Claim a deduction for a decrease in value after the date of death

Sometimes, the fair market value of the property of an unmatured RRSP decreases between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or the estate.

  • If the total of all distributions from the RRSP is less than the fair market value of the property that was included in the deceased annuitant's income for the year of death, the deceased's legal representative can request that the difference be deducted on the deceased's final return through a reassessment.
  • Form RC249 - Post-Death Decline in the Value of a RRIF, an Unmatured RRSP and Post-Death Increase or Decline in the Value of a PRPP can be validated by the RRSP issuer and will allow the executor to claim a deduction on line 23200.
  • Generally, for the deduction to be allowed, the final distribution must occur by December 31 of the year following the death.

Contributions to and withdrawals from the RRSP

Excess contribution

If the deceased individual over-contributed, the executor may need to file a T1-OVP return.

  • What happens if you go over your RRSP deduction limit?
Contributions made after death

If the deceased RRSP holder had remaining contribution room in their RRSP, the executor can contribute to an RRSP held by a surviving spouse or common-law partner and claim the deduction on the deceased's final return 70(1).

Withdrawals

If the deceased individual made withdrawals from the RRSP, there may be tax implications.

  • Making withdrawals from RRSPs

Homebuyer's Plan (HBP) repayments

If the deceased participated in the HBP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP.

The amount of any RRSP, PRPP, SPP, or contributions that the deceased made to their individual plans in the year of their death can be designated as a repayment.

Include on line 12900 the total of all amounts that remained to be repaid at the time of death, unless the legal representative and the surviving spouse or common-law partner jointly elect to have the surviving spouse or common-law partner continue to make the repayments.

  • Deceased participated in the Home Buyers' Plan (HBP).

Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP) repayments

If the deceased participated in the LLP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP.

Treatment of these amounts is the same as with the Home Buyer's Plan, and a similar election is available.

  • Repayments under the Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP).

Matured RRSP

Income up to the date of death

Found on slip
T4RSP - box 16 (issued to the deceased annuitant)
Report on line
12900 of the deceased annuitant's Final return 70(1)

All amounts the annuitant received from the RRSP in the year of death will have to be reported on the annuitant’s final return 70(1). A T4RSP slip is issued in the name of the annuitant for the year of death and will also show any other amounts the annuitant received in the year.

In addition to income from the RRSP, Box 34 of the T4RSP will also show the value of the RRSP on the date of death, but this might not need to be reported with income from the final year, if it was taken over by a spouse or common-law partner, or transferred to a qualifying survivor.

Remaining value of the RRSP on the date of death and transfers

At the time of the annuitant’s death, we consider them to have received the fair market value of all remaining annuity payments held in their matured RRSP.

You can partially or completely reduce the amount of income that is reported on the RRSP holder's final return 70(1) if amounts from the RRSP are transferred to the registered plans of beneficiaries, or if the matured RRSP is taken over by a spouse or common-law partner as the successor annuitant.

A transfer can only be made to a beneficiary who was a financially dependent child or grandchild of the annuitant on the date of death.

Determine how the RRSP is being left to beneficiaries or the estate

Is the RRSP being taken over or transferred?

Yes - taken over by a spouse or common-law partner (successor annuitant)
Found on slip
T4RSP - box 16 (issued to the successor annuitant)
Report on line
12900 of the successor annuitant's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

If the annuitant's spouse or common-law partner is designated as a successor annuitant to all or part of the RRSP in the RRSP contract or the will:

  • the amount of the RRSP that is rolled over to the successor annuitant will not be reported in the final return of the deceased
  • the spouse or common-law partner will begin receiving the remaining annuity payments from the plan and will report those as part of their own income

If a successor annuitant is not identified in the RRSP contract:

If the deceased's will states that the spouse or common-law partner is entitled to the amounts paid under the RRSP, or that the spouse or common-law partner is the sole beneficiary of the estate, that person and the legal representative can jointly elect, in a written letter, to treat the amounts paid to the estate as being paid to the spouse or common-law partner.

  • The written election has to specify that this person is electing to become the annuitant of the RRSP
  • Attach a copy of the written election to the income tax return of the surviving spouse or common-law partner
  • Provide a copy of the written election to the payer of the annuity

    After the election is made, the spouse or common-law partner will be treated as if they were the beneficiary designated in the RRSP contract.

    If a qualifying survivor receives an amount from a deceased annuitant's unmatured RRSP that qualifies as a refund of premiums, the annuitant's legal representative can claim a reduction to the amount that the annuitant is considered to have received at the time of death.

    What qualifies as refund of premiums

    Amounts transferred from the RRSP to a qualifying survivor, up to:

    • the fair market value of the RRSP on the day the annuitant died
    • generally, income earned in the RRSP from the day after the annuitant died to the date the RRSP property is distributed

    For more information on refund of premiums, refer to Chart 1 - Amounts paid from a deceased annuitant's RRSP

    If these amounts are paid to the annuitant's estate, they will qualify as a refund of premiums if both of the following conditions are met:

    • there is a qualifying survivor who is a beneficiary of the annuitant's estate
    • the annuitant's legal representative and the qualifying survivor jointly file Form T2019, Death of an RRSP Annuitant- Refund of Premiums, to designate all or part of the amounts paid to the estate as a refund of premiums received by the qualifying survivor
    Which children qualify as financially dependent

    A child or grandchild of an annuitant, where before the annuitant's death, were living with them and met 1 of the following conditions:

    • net income for the previous year (line 23600 of income tax return) was less than the basic personal amount (line 30000 of income tax return) for that previous year
    • financial dependence is due to a mental or physical infirmity and your net income for the previous year was equal to or less than the basic personal amount, plus the disability amount (line 31600 of income tax return) for that previous year

    The reduction, which is determined by filling out Chart 2, allows for a redistribution of the annuitant's income to the qualifying survivor who actually received it. This redistribution of income allows the deceased annuitant and the qualifying survivor to pay the least amount of tax the law allows.

    When a qualifying survivor includes a refund of premiums in income, they can defer paying tax on the amount by transferring it to an RRSP, PRPP, SPP, RRIF, RDSP or to buy an eligible annuity.

    • Transfers that qualifying survivors can choose

    The transfer must be completed in the year the refund of premiums is received or within 60 days after the end of the year.

    If the qualifying survivor is 71 in the year the refund of premiums is received, the transfer to an RRSP must be completed by December 31 of that year.

    The issuer who receives the transferred funds will issue a receipt to the qualifying survivor. The qualifying survivor can use the receipt to claim a deduction on their income tax return for the year the refund of premiums was received.

    Rollover to an RDSP
    Found on slip
    T4A - box 131 (issued to the beneficiary)
    Report on line
    12500 of the beneficiary's T1 Income Tax and Benefit return

    The existing RRSP rollover rules allow a specified RDSP payment from the deceased person's RRSP to the RDSP of an eligible individual.

    Who qualifies as an eligible individual

    A child or grandchild of a deceased annuitant under an RRSP or a RRIF, or of a deceased member of a Registered Pension Plan (RPP), PRPP, or SPP who was financially dependent on the deceased for support, at the time of the deceased's death, because of an impairment in physical or mental function.

    The eligible individual must also be the beneficiary under the RDSP into which the eligible proceeds will be paid.

    A specified RDSP payment is a payment that meets all of the following conditions:

    • the payment is made to an RDSP under which the eligible individual is the beneficiary
    • the payment satisfies the contribution conditions described in the Registered Disability Savings Plan
    • effective March 19, 2019, if the eligible individual no longer qualifies for the disability tax credit, the payment is made before the end of the fifth taxation year throughout which the beneficiary is ineligible for the disability tax credit

    Form RC4625, Rollover to a Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP) Under Paragraph 60(m) must be attached to both the deceased annuitant's and the eligible individual's income tax and benefit returns.

    In these situations, you will not have to fill out a Schedule 7, RRSP, PRPP, and SPP Unused Contributions, Transfers, and HBP or LLP Activities. However, you must attach to the income tax and benefit return the receipt indicating the amount of the rollover.

    The amount that can be rolled over to an RDSP cannot exceed the RDSP lifetime limit of $200,000.

    Deductions beneficiaries can claim

    Deduction for elected split-pension amounts with a spouse or common-law partner

    If the deceased received RRSP annuity payments before the date of death, this income can be treated as pension income for purposes of the deduction for elected split pension amount. This deduction allows this income to be split between the deceased and their spouse or common law partner, which could reduce the total amount of tax payable.

    To make this election, complete Form T1032, Joint Election to Split Pension Income. Enter the full amount of the annuity payments received on line 12900 of the deceased’s final return 70(1), and claim a deduction for the elected split-pension amount on line 21000.

    The spouse or common-law partner of the deceased will need to report their split-pension amount on line 12900 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return.

    • Line 21000 - Deduction for elected split-pension amount
    Deduction for amounts rolled over or transferred
    • If the property was directly transferred to a beneficiary's RRSP, PRPP or SPP, they can claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 20800 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return
    • If the amount was transferred to their RRIF, RDSP or annuity, claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 23200 of their T1 Income Tax and Benefit return
