Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP)

How an RRSP is treated at the time of the annuitant’s death depends on whether it is matured or unmatured and if any income was earned after the date of death.

You may need to contact your financial institution or investment broker to determine which type of RRSP the deceased held.

On this page

  • Determine the type of RRSP
  • Repayments to a Homebuyer's Plan (HBP) or Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)

Determine the type of RRSP

  • Matured RRSP: Option 2

    A plan you can no longer contribute to.

    Its purpose is to provide retirement income for its beneficiaries.

    There are no tax implications whether you withdrawl funds or not.

  • Unmatured RRSP: Option 1

    A retirement savings and investment plan for individuals to contribute to.

    It has not yet started to pay the retirement income to the annuitant.

    At age 71, the unmatured RRSP must be converted to an RRIF or buy an annuity.

Matured RRSP

Report on: Income tax and benefit return - line 12900 (of the deceased annuitant)

The RRSP payments the deceased received from January 1 to the date of death must be reported.

Is the spouse or common-law partner designated as the beneficiary of the registered plan in the RRSP contract or will?

yes - rollover to a spouse or common-law partner

Report on: Income tax and benefit return - line 12900 (of the spouse or common-law partner)

If the annuitant's spouse or common-law partner is designated as a beneficiary of the RRSP, in the RRSP contract or the will, they will begin receiving the remaining annuity payments from the plan.

no - transfer to another beneficiary or the estate

Found on: T4RSP slip - box 34 (issued to the deceased annuitant)

Reported on: Income tax and benefit return - line 12900 (of the deceased annuitant's final return)

no-elect to have RRSP payments continue to be paid to the spouse or common-law partner

If the surviving spouse or common-law partner is the beneficiary of the estate, that person and the legal representative can jointly elect, in writing, to treat the amounts the RRSP paid to the estate as being paid to the spouse or common-law partner.

  • Attach a copy of the written election to the return of the surviving spouse or common-law partner. The election has to specify that this person is electing to become the annuitant of the RRSP.
Unmatured RRSP

Found on: T4RSP slip - box 34 (issued to the deceased annuitant)

Reported on: Income tax and benefit return - line 12900 (of deceased annuitant's final return)

Generally, the CRA considers a deceased annuitant to have received, immediately before death, an amount equal to the fair market value (FMV) of all the property of the unmatured plan at the time of death. The FMV is shown on the T4RSP slip.

Is the spouse or common-law partner, or an RDSP account designated as the beneficiary of the registered plan in the RRSP contract or will?

yes - rollover to a spouse or common-law partner

Found on: T4RIF slip - box 16 (issued to spouse or common-law partner)

Reported on: Income tax and benefit return - line 11500 (of spouse or common-law partner)

2 conditions must be met:

  • spouse or common-law partner is named as sole beneficiary of the RRSP contract, and
  • entire RRSP property is directly transferred, by December 31 of the year following the year of death, into a registered plan (RRSP, PRPP SPP, or RRIF) in which the spouse or common-law partner is the annuitant, or to an issuer to buy an eligible annuity.

  • Claim the deduction
    • If the property was directly transferred to the spouse or common-law partner’s RRSP, PRPP or SPP, they can claim a deduction on line 20800 of their Income tax and benefit return.
    • If the amount was transferred to their RRIF, RDSP or annuity, claim a deduction equal to the amount transferred, on line 23200 of their Income tax and benefit return.

    • Example scenario

      Martin died in June 2019 at the age of 67. When he died the FMV of his unmatured trusteed RRSP was $185,000. The FMV of the RRSP on December 31, 2020, was $215,000. On June 30, 2021, the day the RRSP property was distributed, the FMV of the RRSP was $225,000.

      The RRSP contract designated Martin's spouse, Elaine, as the sole beneficiary. Elaine, who is also the legal representative of Martin's estate, received the following two slips from the RRSP issuer:

      • a T4RSP slip for 2021 issued in her name, showing $30,000 in box 18, and $10,000 in boxes 28 and 40
      • a T4RSP slip for 2019 in Martin's name, showing $185,000 in box 34. Although Elaine is the sole beneficiary, the slip was issued to Martin because the conditions were not met.

      Elaine wants to know if it would be beneficial to ask for a reduction to the amount that we consider Martin received from his RRSP when he died. She fills out Chart 2, and determines that she can claim a reduction of $185,000. She reviews Martin's tax situation and her own, and decides to claim a $100,000 reduction. This reduces the amount reported on line 12900 of Martin's 2019 income tax and benefit return to $85,000 ($185,000 – $100,000), and increases the amount reported on line 12900 of Elaine's 2021 income tax and benefit return to $140,000 ($100,000 + $30,000 + $10,000).

      Because the FMV of the RRSP at the time of death was included in Martin's income for 2019, Elaine has to write a letter to ask for an adjustment to that year's income tax and benefit return. To minimize her 2021 taxes, she transfers $130,000 to her RRIF. This is the difference between the amount she included in income ($140,000) and the amount shown in boxes 28 and 40 of her T4RSP slip ($10,000). Elaine claims a $130,000 deduction on line 23200 of her 2021 income tax and benefit return.

yes - rollover to a Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP)

Rollover of RRSP proceeds to a registered disability savings plan (RDSP) - The existing RRSP rollover rules discussed in the previous section are extended to allow a specified RDSP payment from the deceased person's RRSP to the RDSP of an eligible individual.

An eligible individual is a child or grandchild of a deceased annuitant under an RRSP or a RRIF, or of a deceased member of a registered pension plan (RPP), PRPP, or SPP who was financially dependent on the deceased for support at the time of the deceased's death, because of an impairment in physical or mental function.

A specified RDSP payment is a payment that meets all of the following conditions:

  • the payment is made to an RDSP under which the eligible individual is the beneficiary
  • the payment satisfies the contribution conditions described in Guide RC4460, Registered Disability Savings Plan
  • effective March 19, 2019, if the eligible individual no longer qualifies for the disability tax credit, the payment is made before the end of the fifth taxation year throughout which the beneficiary is ineligible for the disability tax credit 

For more information, go to Registered disability savings plan (RDSP) or see Information Sheet RC4177, Death of an RRSP Annuitant.

no - transfer to another beneficiary or the estate

At the time of the annuitant’s death, the FMV of all property held in the unmatured plan is included in their income for the year of death. The T4RSP slip will be issued in the name of the deceased annuitant with an amount in box 34, and their income has to be reported on their final Income tax and benefit return.

When the beneficiary receives income earned in the RRSP after the annuitant dies, a T4RSP slip is issued in their name, with an amount shown in box 28. This amount is to be reported by the beneficiary on their Income tax and benefit return.

When the estate receives income earned in the RRSP after the annuitant dies, a T4RSP slip is issued in their name, with an amount shown in box 28. This amount is to be reported by the estate.

The amount reported on the deceased annuitant's final income tax return might be reduced if an amount is paid from the unmatured RRSP to either you or the estate of which you are the beneficiary. At the time of death, you must be either:

  • the spouse or common-law partner of the annuitant
  • a financially dependant child or grandchild of the annuitant
    • no-elect to have RRSP payments continue to be paid to the spouse or common-law partner

    If the surviving spouse or common-law partner is the beneficiary of the estate, that person and the legal representative can jointly elect, in writing, to treat the amounts the RRSP paid to the estate as being paid to the spouse or common-law partner.

    Attach a copy of the written election to the return of the surviving spouse or common-law partner. The election has to specify that this person is electing to become the annuitant of the RRSP.

    Did the value of the registered account increase or decrease between the date of death and when it was paid to a beneficiary?

    yes - increased in value

    Sometimes there is an increase in the value of an RRSP between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or estate.

    • This amount has to be included in the income of the beneficiary or the estate for the year it is received. A T4RSP slip will be issued for this amount.

    For more information, see Chart 6 - Amounts from a deceased annuitant's RRSP, in Chapter 5 of Guide T4040, RRSPs and Other Registered Plans for Retirement.

    yes - decreased in value

    Sometimes, the FMV of the property of an unmatured RRSP decreases between the date of death and the date of final distribution to the beneficiary or the estate.

    • If the total of all distributions from the RRSP is less than the FMV of the property that was included in the deceased annuitant's income for the year of death, the deceased's legal representative can request that the difference be deducted on the deceased's final return. Generally, for the deduction to be allowed, the final distribution must occur by the end of the year that follows the year of death.

    For more information, see Information Sheet RC4177, Death of an RRSP Annuitant.

    no - there was no change in value

    If the value of the RRSP did not change between the date of death and distribution to a beneficiary, there are no further amounts to report.

    Deduction for elected split-amounts with a spouse or common-law partner

    If the deceased person jointly elected with their spouse or common-law partner to split RRSP annuity payments that the pensioner received up until the date of death and entered on line 12900, the elected split-pension amount can be deducted on line 21000. For more information, see Line 21000 - Deduction for elected split-pension amount.

    Repayments to a Homebuyer's Plan (HBP) or Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)

    These will only apply to an unmatured RRSP.

    • Home Buyer's Plan (HBP)

      If the deceased participated in the HBP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP.

      In this case, include on line 12900 the total of all amounts that remain to be repaid at the time of death. The amount of any RRSP, PRPP, SPP, or contributions that the deceased made to their individual plans in the year of their death can be designated as a repayment.

      However, you do not have to report these amounts when the legal representative and the surviving spouse or common-law partner jointly elect to have the surviving spouse or common-law partner continue to make the repayments.

      For more information, see Home Buyers' Plan (HBP).

      • Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)

        If the deceased participated in the LLP, they would have made a withdrawal from their RRSP and may have been making repayments to their RRSP, PRPP, or SPP. Treatment of these amounts is the same as with the Home Buyer's Plan, and a similar election is available.

        For more information, see Guide RC4112, Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP).
