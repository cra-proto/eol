Belongings and property

All real assets as well as certain investments like mutual funds and GICs held in unregistered accounts are deemed disposed upon the death of the individual and they must be addressed for capital gains and losses for your tax returns.

On this page

  • Determine if property is rolled over to the surviving spouse

Determine if property is rolled over to the surviving spouse

If the spouse is the beneficiary: Option 1 of 2

Any assets or property that are transferred within 36 months after the date of death to a surviving spouse or common-law partner that is resident in Canada can be excluded from the final returns.

This effectively delays the tax implications until the spouse’s death.

If you choose not to use the spousal rollover or transfer assets after the 36 month deadline, you must assess them like all other transfers for capital gains.

If property or assets are not being transferred through spousal rollover: Option 2 of 2

Any property or assets will need to be assessed for capital gains in the final return.

Refer to the sections below on how to report types of capital gains and income on the final return.

Special circumstances

If you need more than 36 months

If you, as the legal representative of the deceased, cannot meet the 36 month condition in time, a request for an extension may be considered if you:

  • write a request to the director at your Tax Services Office
  • before the end of the 36-month time period
Transfer to a testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust

You may alternative make a rollover to a trust if:

  • The testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust is resident in Canada right after the property becomes locked-in for this trust.
  • The property becomes locked-in for the testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust no later than 36 months after the date of death. If you need more time to meet this condition, you can make a written request to the director at your tax services office before the end of the 36-month time period.

Assessing property for capital gains

Principal residence

Must designate as principal residence in the personal income tax return

Although gains on a principal residence can be exempt, you must still designate the principal residence in your return by completing both:

  • page 2 in Schedule 3, Capital Gains (or Losses) in 2021
  • Form T1255, Designation of a Property as a Principal Residence by the Legal Representative of a Deceased Individual

Special circumstances

Vacancy or no longer principal residence of beneficiary after death

If the property remains vacant after the death, or is no longer the principal resident of the beneficiary, it will become taxable. Report gains in value after the date of death on a T3 trust income tax return.

Transfer to spouse or common-law partner

Where the principal residence is transferred to the deceased’s spouse, common-law partner, or testamentary spousal or common-law partner trust (under the conditions described in the next section), the CRA does not require you to make a principal residence designation for the period before death. Instead, the disposition must be reported and the designation may be made by the surviving spouse, common-law partner, or the trust at the earlier of the time of actual disposition or death of the surviving spouse.

A record should be kept of the years for which the deceased individual would have been eligible to make a principal residence designation for the particular property.

Farm and fishing land and supplies

Generally personal income tax return (special cases apply)

If you have land or assets associated with farm or fishing that generated business income, you may be eligible to treat this as business farm or fishing property. Refer to farm or fishing property and equipment.

Sales of property or assets after the death

T3 trust income tax return

If any property, investments or items are sold after the date of death, including the principal residence, before being passed onto a beneficiary, you must report the capital gain or loss in a T3 return for the estate.

Since TFSAs and registered plans are no longer active after the date of death, any investments that were inside these plans must also be included in capital gains after the date of death.
